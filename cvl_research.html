OVERVIEW

The following contains a brief overview of both my scholarly research interests as well as selected areas of continuing professional journalistic and digital media innovation I would bring to the Herbert School –– each of which, most importantly, would compliment my most primary duty as an assistant professor: teaching.

I. Data visualization and information design

As both a practitioner and a thought-leader in the areas of data visualization and information design, my research spans various aspects of the emerging field, including human-centered design (HCD), distilling multivariate datasets for viewing on mobile and wearable devices, data visualization ethics, design thinking and the process of finding meaning out of the almost infinite amount of Big Data that proliferates our lives in the 21st Century. Following the work of seminal thinkers and academics in the field of information design such as Edward Tufte, to prolific programmers who have rapidly sped up the process of visualizing large datasets such as d3.js creator Mike Bostock, my interest in information visualization aims both to be theoretical (Tufte-like) and practical (solving current technical or programmatic problems, much as Bostock has).

II. “Post-platform” and other emerging journalistic paradigms

  • [ ] In an era of almost limitless and ever-expanding platforms for mass communication, it remains important to remember that today’s platforms may be tomorrow’s printing press, and that ultimately all communications technologies are, ipso facto, ephemeral in nature. In previous research, I’ve explored this notion through the lens of the journalist’s self-identification based upon the tools in which he or she specializes. From “print journalist”, to “data journalist”, to “multimedia journalist” and even to “social journalist,” it seems as though the qualifiers that often get placed before the word “journalist” abound in almost cliche numbers. Each of these “types” of journalism typically seek to clarify the platform in which the individual tells the story rather than the content of the story itself. But in an era of information abundance and the democratization of publishing, we’ve seen the rapid rise of almost limitless numbers of platforms that require a range of almost limitless skills. Hence the need for a deeper understanding among young journalists to adapt to the role of what I call the “post-platform” journalist – one not tethered to a particular technology or medium, but rather agile and responsive to the needs of the journalist’s particular audience.

As what Columbia University’s Tow Center Director Emily Bell calls the ”post-industrial age of journalism” comes into full reality, other scholars and practitioners have sought to create or reignite similar medium-agnostic paradigms to support the journalistic work they do. Such paradigms include collaborative journalism, precision journalism, civic journalism, advocacy journalism, constructive journalism, entrepreneurial journalism, data journalism, open-source journalism and social journalism, just to name a few. Exploring the inherently human need to self-identify that accompanies these new media paradigms is an area of research deserving of scholarly attention.

III. Digital security and source protection

In today’s post-Snowden media landscape, it is more crucial than ever that journalists be able to communicate with and protect their sources’ identities by affording them the privacy traditionally bestowed upon them by the Fourth Estate. But the rapid advance in surveillance technologies developed by government entities such as the NSA threaten to tear away that fundamental right of journalists that has been consistently upheld by American courts for decades.

IV. Funding journalism in a post-advertising era

Funding digital journalism in the mobile and increasingly wearable age of communications –– particularly at the local and state levels –– is of paramount importance in creating and sustaining informed and engaged citizens. of particular interest is a constructivist approach to discovering sustainable funding sources for local news coverage in the post-advertising era – likely through some combination of consumer membership revenue, collaborations with institutions such as Universities and libraries, foundational support, and even public subsidies. Furthermore, developing more sophisticated ”impact metrics” and exploring the perils of a single-metric (i.e., page views) system of self-evaluation rank high among my interests as a media scholar.

CONCLUDING

Together, I employ a cross-disciplinary approach to communications studies that spans the disciplines of statistics, computer science, digital humanities, service-learning, interaction design, mobile development, industrial-era journalism practices, urbanism, data science and emerging storytelling paradigms. The logic behind such a holistic approach to communications research is patent: Digital literacy and user engagement are of paramount interest not only to journalists, but to professionals in almost all disciplines.
